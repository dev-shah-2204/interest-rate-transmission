Repo Rate: The rate at which central banks lend short-term funds to commercial banks in exchange for government securities. It helps control liquidity and inflation in the economy, influencing other interest rates.

Certificate of Deposit: A fixed-term deposit issued by banks, offering a specified interest rate and maturity date; low-risk and non-negotiable.

Commercial Paper: A short-term, unsecured promissory note issued by corporations to raise funds for working capital, usually with a maturity of up to 270 days.

365-Day Bill Yield (percent): The annualized yield of a 365-day government treasury bill, reflecting the return as a percentage of the face value.

Loan: A sum of money borrowed from a bank or financial institution with an agreed interest rate, repayable over a specified term.

Term Deposit (>1 year): A fixed deposit with a tenure exceeding one year, offering higher interest rates in exchange for reduced liquidity.

Saving Deposit: A deposit account with a bank that earns interest, providing easy access to funds while encouraging savings.

Call Rate (Percent per annum): The interest rate at which banks lend and borrow overnight funds from each other to meet short-term liquidity needs.
